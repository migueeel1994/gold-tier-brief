Section 1

Context

What's driving this brief — and what one thing needs to change?

A target you're missing, a competitor move, a launch, a regulatory change, a customer behaviour shift. One paragraph. No generic ambition.
Problem or Opportunity Pick one — not both. This is a hard rule for Gold Tier.
Template: "The single thing that needs to change is ___ because ___."
⚠ Gate
If your answer contains "and" or "&", you may be hiding two problems. Cut it to one.
Section 2

Objectives — linked cascade

Three levels, all connected. Each must trigger the one above it.

Commercial objective Fill all four. They auto-compose into a S.M.A.R.T. statement.
What specifically do we need the audience to DO? Verb-specific — open an account, switch from X, add to existing holding, refer.
Attitudinal objective Frame as a shift, not a snapshot.
⚠ Cascade check
Walk the chain out loud: if the audience FEELS [attitudinal], will they DO [behavioural]? If they DO [behavioural], does [commercial] follow? If any link breaks, the strategy isn't ready.
Section 3

Audience

Sharp enough to picture one person. "Australians 25–55" fails.

Pick an existing persona
Age, gender, income, occupation, geography.
Worldview, values, what they care about. Plain language.
How, why, where, when and how often do they engage with the category or brand?
Loyal, lapsed, competitor user, category non-user. Name competitors specifically.
Section 4

Insight + Message

The hardest section. The most important. Insights reveal tensions — they aren't statistics.

What's the single belief or behaviour blocking the commercial outcome? Give the evidence — research, sales data, customer feedback, behavioural pattern.
⚠ Gate
If your answer starts with "People…", "Research shows…", "Most Australians…" — it's an observation, not an insight. Ask: what does that belief make them do, or avoid doing, that they secretly wish was different?
Behavioural insight The barrier (why isn't this happening today?) and the lever (what removes it?).
Which principle maps to the barrier — framing, social proof, default effect, loss aversion, reciprocity, scarcity, source effect, anchoring? Pick one or two. Don't list seven.
One sentence. Sets the creative direction. Don't write the line.
⚠ Gates on the message
  • FANBOYS scan — if the message contains for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so, it may be saying two things.
  • Length — over 25 words is amber. Compress.
  • Single-minded test — read it aloud. Can you cut half the words and keep the point? If yes, do.
Reasons to believe In hierarchy: hard facts > customer evidence > brand claims. Adjectives aren't proof.

Section 5

Creative Guidance

Help creative understand the emotional territory — don't prescribe the solution.

What emotional response should the work produce? Not aesthetic ("modern", "premium") — emotional ("calm capability", "recognition", "permission to start").
Tones, claims or territories the creative must NOT go near. Be specific — "don't be boring" doesn't help.
Mood, not prescription. "Make it like the Apple ad" is prescription. "We admire the calm authority of this work" is mood.
